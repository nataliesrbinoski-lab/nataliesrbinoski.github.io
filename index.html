Theme 1 · Employability

Finding the Intersection:
How Pharmacy and Neuroscience Led Me to Neuropharmacology

Where I Started

I did not plan to be interested in neuropharmacology. I planned to be interested in pharmacy. And separately, neuroscience. It took most of my degree to realise those two things were the same plan all along.

The questions that kept coming up in both fields were the same ones. How do drugs cross the blood-brain barrier? How do receptor dynamics translate into clinical outcomes? Why do compounds with strong in vitro activity sometimes fail entirely in the CNS?

Neuropharmacology is where those questions are answered. Recognising that intersection was the first step. Knowing what to do with it is the work I am still doing.

What I Did This Semester

During CHEM324 I researched my pathway concretely. I am already enrolled in Honours at UOW beginning next year. The key question is not whether to do Honours, but what direction to take it in.

I reviewed neuropharmacology-adjacent research groups at UOW through the Molecular Horizons Institute, looked into the Molecular Neurosciences research theme, and researched the Master of Pharmacy at the University of Sydney as my intended registration pathway after Honours.

What CHEM324 Changed

What I did not expect was how much the practical work of CHEM324 would clarify my thinking. The Cu(II) peptide project required designing experiments around an open research question, interpreting spectroscopic data, and drawing conclusions from incomplete evidence. That is exactly what Honours research demands.

The water analysis project contributed differently. Managing a complex multi-analyte dataset and being required to classify unknown samples with explicit confidence levels felt directly relevant to the evidence-based reasoning that pharmacy practice also requires.

Both projects, in different ways, confirmed that the path I have chosen suits how I think.

Key development

Moved from having two separate interests to understanding how they connect, and from general curiosity to a concrete three-step plan: Honours at UOW, MPharm at USYD, pharmacy registration.

Theme 2 · Research Skills

From Raw Data to
Chemical Inference

The Problem With Just Reporting Numbers

For the first few weeks of Project 1, I thought data analysis meant producing results. It took a physically impossible number to make me realise it actually meant interrogating them.

Project 1 required analysis of six unknown water samples across pH, conductivity, TSS, TDS, nutrients, anions, major ions, and trace metals by both UV-Vis and MP-AES. Early in the project I treated individual measurements as conclusions. A conductivity value was a result. A metal concentration was a result.

What I had not developed yet was the habit of asking what those results meant together.

When the Data Did Not Make Sense

The shift happened gradually. When dissolved copper exceeded total leachable copper in several samples, which is physically difficult to explain, I had to decide whether to flag the anomaly or ignore it.

I flagged it. I attributed the discrepancy to sub-sample heterogeneity or matrix effects, noted it as a limitation, and moved on. That decision, small as it sounds, was a shift in how I thought about data.

The same thing happened with Sample F. Its sulphate and magnesium concentrations exceeded those of Sample A, the marine endmember. A simple seawater dilution model could not explain that. I said so explicitly in the report rather than forcing a clean classification onto ambiguous evidence.

"Accepting uncertainty rather than forcing a clean answer was harder than it sounds. But it was also the most important analytical skill I developed this semester."

Theme 3 · Research Skills

Precision in Practice:
Learning Through What Went Wrong

What I Expected vs What I Found

Our Cu(II)-cysteine complex came out dark purple. The literature said it should be pink. That was the moment CHEM324 stopped feeling like a subject and started feeling like actual research.

I came into this semester expecting laboratory work to be largely procedural. Follow the method. Obtain the result. What I found was that the method is a starting point, not a guarantee.

Responding well to unexpected outcomes turned out to be just as important as following instructions correctly.

Case Study 1 — When the Numbers Were Unreliable

In Practical 3 (MP-AES), copper blank contamination accounted for 70.8% of the total leachable sample concentration. Iron showed an RSD exceeding 15% between duplicates.

These were not minor uncertainties. They meaningfully compromised the reliability of those measurements.

In my e-lab book I reported them honestly and proposed specific fixes: higher purity reagents, acid-washed glassware, and additional low-concentration calibration points. At the time, writing those reflections felt like admitting failure. I now recognise that identifying and communicating the limitations of your data is a core competency in analytical chemistry, not an admission of incompetence.

"At the time, writing those reflections felt like admitting failure. Now I understand it as one of the most important things a scientist can do."

Case Study 2 — When the Synthesis Did Not Match the Literature

The yield exceeded 100%. That pointed immediately to impurities or incomplete drying rather than a clean product. We trialled four different solvents trying to dissolve it adequately, researched recrystallisation approaches, and cross-referenced FTIR data to assess whether Cu coordination had actually occurred.

None of this was in the original method. It required treating the problem as a research question rather than a procedural failure.

The Detective Work

The most significant shift in my thinking this semester came from piecing together three separate lines of evidence: the MS/MS fragment ions, the weakened S-H band in the FTIR overlay, and the GFAAS copper content.

Each technique added a partial answer. The full picture only emerged when I considered them together.

That experience changed how I understand characterisation. It is not confirmation of a known result. It is evidence-based reasoning under uncertainty, and it is exactly the kind of thinking that independent Honours research will require.

Key development

Moved from following a method to diagnosing why a method failed, and from treating unexpected results as errors to treating them as information.

Next step

Carry this mindset into Honours, where troubleshooting will be the norm rather than the exception.

Theme 4 · Research Skills

Communicating Chemistry:
From Written Report to Academic Presentation

Two Formats, Two Different Challenges

Writing the water analysis report and presenting the Cu(II) poster to four academics required the same information. But they were completely different challenges. One rewarded precision. The other rewarded the ability to think out loud.

The written report required a structured argument from evidence: move from raw data through interpretation to conclusions, with every claim supported and every limitation acknowledged.

The ePoster required me to distil that same complexity into a visual format and then defend it verbally, on the spot, to academics who could ask anything.

What Writing Taught Me

One aspect of the report I found genuinely difficult was knowing how much uncertainty to express. I classified Sample C as a disturbed urban nitrogen system with low confidence and said so explicitly, because the evidence was genuinely ambiguous.

I was uncertain whether acknowledging that uncertainty would be seen as weakness. The marking rubric rewarded it. That reinforced something I will carry forward: scientific writing is not about projecting certainty. It is about accurately representing what the evidence does and does not support.

Using this rubric actively while drafting my report helped me understand the difference between describing data and analysing it. What Presenting Taught Me

Four academic staff members came to our poster individually, asked questions, and expected us to respond without a script. Feedback noted that the graphs were well-presented and that the MS/MS labelling and overlaid IR spectra were particularly effective.

I was also told to step back from the script and speak more conversationally. That feedback was accurate. I had prepared thoroughly, but preparation had tipped into over-rehearsal. The delivery was technically correct but not naturally confident.

"One academic asked whether I genuinely believed peptide complexation was worth pursuing given our results. I had to think on my feet. The honest answer was better than anything I had scripted."

The honest answer was that results under disc diffusion conditions were limited, but the question of whether peptide coordination could improve copper bioavailability or selectivity remained scientifically interesting. Giving that answer, unrehearsed, was more valuable than any rehearsed version of it would have been.

Theme 5 · Research Skills

Research as a Team:
Lessons from the Cu(II) Complex Project

A Different Kind of Group Work

Group work in a research context is different from group work in a taught course. The stakes feel different when nobody knows the answer in advance, including the demonstrators.

Project 2 asked a genuinely open question: can Cu(II)-cysteine and Cu(II)-peptide complexes demonstrate antibacterial activity, and does peptide coordination improve on copper salts alone?

Producing a credible response required coordinating synthesis, characterisation, and biological testing across a group, under time pressure, with results that did not always behave as expected.

What Collaboration Actually Looked Like

When our Cu(II)-cysteine synthesis produced a dark purple product at greater than 100% yield, the group had to agree on how to interpret and represent that result honestly in the poster. That required everyone to justify their reasoning, not just divide up tasks.

The moments of disagreement within the group about how confidently to state a conclusion were actually productive. They forced more careful thinking about what the evidence actually supported.

The poster discussion section, which honestly assessed the limitations of the disc diffusion assay and the role of solubility in limiting antibacterial activity, was stronger because of that process.

"The hardest part of the collaboration was not the chemistry. It was agreeing on how confidently to state something when the evidence was incomplete."

My Contribution

My contribution focused on characterisation analysis and written communication. Interpreting the FTIR overlay, cross-referencing the MS/MS fragment ions, and drafting the discussion section were the parts of the project where I felt most confident and most engaged.

I also found that explaining my interpretation to group members, and having them push back on it, made my own understanding more precise.

What I Will Take Forward

Pharmaceutical research, clinical teams, and Honours supervision all involve sustained collaboration on problems without clean answers.

CHEM324 gave me an early experience of what that actually feels like, including the frustration when things do not work, the iteration required to find an explanation, and the satisfaction when a coherent picture finally emerges from separate lines of evidence.

Key development

Moved from thinking of group work as task division to understanding it as collective reasoning, where disagreement and justification are part of the process.

Next step

Approach Honours as a collaborative relationship with a supervisor, not a solo task with occasional check-ins.
